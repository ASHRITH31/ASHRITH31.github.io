Writing

Backbone networking,
explained from the inside.

March 11, 2026 · First Post
Last year, I walked into Airtel's Cable Landing Station in Chennai. I didn't fully understand what I was about to see.
Somewhere on the ocean floor, a cable thinner than a garden hose is carrying 95% of the world's intercontinental internet traffic — including the message you're reading right now. Every 60-80km along that cable, a repeater the size of a torpedo amplifies the signal. Powered by ±10,000 volts of constant current sent from the shore. Through thousands of kilometres of ocean.

Inside the station, I watched engineers read fault signals — AIS, BDI, LOS — the language cables use when something goes wrong. I saw DWDM systems multiplexing hundreds of wavelengths down a single fiber, each one carrying a different stream of data. I held the fiber itself. Thinner than a human hair.

I went in expecting to observe. I came out fascinated. That fascination is why I'm now building MiniNet-IX — a simulated internet backbone using GNS3, where I'm configuring BGP between 3 Autonomous Systems from scratch.
